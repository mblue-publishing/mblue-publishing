## Description

You know what happens on that Tuesday.

It's not a dramatic day. You come off a ladder wrong, or you step back from a panel and something in the knee moves the way it shouldn't. Somewhere on the drive home or in a waiting room chair with a clipboard on your lap, you do a piece of arithmetic you've been putting off for years.

What happens to my income if this doesn't come back?

If you're an electrician, plumber, carpenter, or roofer in your 40s or 50s, your income and your physical capacity are the same thing. When the body stops, the work stops. And if you run your own operation, there may be no disability coverage that actually replaces your income during a six-month recovery.

Most tradespeople know they can't do this work at 65. What most don't have is a concrete plan for what comes before that.

That's the gap this book is designed to close.

The Trade-Out Plan is a path-by-path field manual written by a licensed master electrician who came off scaffolding wrong at 46, looked at his $28,000 in savings, and realized eight weeks of disability coverage is not an exit plan. He spent the next five years building one, going through three of the four paths in this book himself. None of it is theoretical.

## Inside this book

There are four exit paths, each built on the credentials and field experience you already have:

- Inspector and Estimator: In many states, a master electrician or licensed plumber is already most of the way to inspector certification. Year-one income ranges from $45,000 to $70,000, with low physical demand and a credential pathway that starts with the license already in your pocket.
- Consulting and Training: Your licensed expertise can generate $500 to $1,200 per day in consulting fees and $1,500 to $4,000 per semester teaching at a community college, built part-time while you're still working full-time.
- Trade Industry Sales: Territory rep roles with building products manufacturers pay $60,000 to $85,000 in year one, come with employer benefits and a company vehicle, and favor a licensed tradesperson's peer-level credibility over anyone who learned the product from a training binder.
- Income Property: A licensed tradesperson who owns a small rental saves an estimated $2,000 to $4,000 per year in contractor costs versus a non-trades investor on the same property, and can accurately assess what a building is actually worth before signing anything.

Each chapter includes real income numbers, specific credential checklists, and a 90-day milestone sequence you can start this weekend while still employed. No business plan. No career counselor. No starting over.

The planning window and the retirement window are not the same thing. For most tradespeople between 40 and 58, the planning window closes first. By years. Every year without a secondary income stream in place is a year you can't get back when the timeline tightens.

By the last page, you'll have a written 12-month plan in hand and one first step already behind you.

That's not a motivational promise. It's a construction schedule.
