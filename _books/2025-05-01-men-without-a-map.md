## Book Description

It probably started on a Saturday.

Coffee going cold at the kitchen table. Phone in front of you. The thought crossing your mind to call someone, followed immediately by the realization that you weren't quite sure who to call.

Not because you'd done anything wrong. Because the friendships you had inside that marriage weren't entirely yours. They were shared infrastructure, jointly maintained, largely managed by your wife. When the marriage ended, that infrastructure went with it.

This is not a grief book. It's a logistics book.

Your social world didn't collapse because you were difficult, or emotionally unavailable, or never particularly well-liked on your own. It collapsed because long marriages divide social labor along predictable lines. The husband handles practical logistics. The wife handles relational infrastructure. Almost nobody notices the arrangement until the marriage ends and the social world vanishes overnight.

That mechanism is documented. The collapse was structural, not personal. And once it's clear, it stops looking like a verdict on who you are and starts looking like a problem with a practical solution.

David Calloway is a former newspaper editor who went through grey divorce at fifty-seven, spent fourteen months performing normalcy while his social world quietly emptied, and then rebuilt it from scratch. Three years later, it's steadier than it was at any point in the marriage. He wrote this book because the field guide he needed didn't exist.

## Inside this book

- Why your couple-friends evaporated completely rather than drifting away gradually, and why the totality of that loss says nothing about whether people actually like you
- What male friendship at sixty actually looks like, why it forms differently than it did at thirty-five, and how to stop measuring progress against the wrong standard
- The exact mechanics of reaching back to someone you haven't spoken to in fifteen years, without explanation, without apology, without it being strange
- A clear framework for finding communities worth your time at this age and becoming part of one, not just a visitor
- How to build a week that generates genuine contact rather than the performance of a social life
- How to be a steady presence for your adult children without becoming a quiet burden
- How to navigate the couple-dominated social spaces you inherited from the marriage, and why they shouldn't be the center of your social life anymore

No therapy language. No journal prompts. No healing exercises. Nothing that requires you to identify as someone who needs help. The book can be read in a weekend.

You don't need to become a different person. You need a map.
